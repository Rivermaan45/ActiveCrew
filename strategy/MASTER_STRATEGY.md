### Key Decisions Made (v0.5.0)

**3 Launch Sports (data-driven):**
1. **Running** — #1 participation sport in US. NYC has massive run club culture (NYRR 60K+ members). Free. Year-round. Any group size.
2. **Tennis** — 20%+ growth since 2019. Skill matching is THE use case. Public courts everywhere. 2-4 people. Court booking = revenue opportunity.
3. **Padel** — Fastest growing racquet sport globally. Always doubles (inherently 4-person group = social). Appeals to international transplants (ActiveCrew's core demo). Multiple clubs opening in NYC.

*Other sports (surfing, hiking, cycling, yoga, climbing, gym) kept in notes for future expansion after proving PMF with 3.*

**Color: Emerald Green (#10b981)** — replaced orange. Based on UX color psychology research:
- Orange = Strava energy. Made ActiveCrew look like a fitness tracker.
- Emerald green = health, growth, nature, trust, fresh start. Sporty without being aggressive.
- Distinctive from ALL competitors: Bumble (yellow), Hinge (dark), Tinder (red), Strava (orange).
- Studies show green boosts energy and signals health/wellness (Journal of Environmental Psychology, 2010).

## 1. Is This Idea Good?

**Verdict: 7.5/10 — Strong idea, real problem. Risk is execution, not concept.**

### The Problem

- **30% of US adults** report regular loneliness (APA, 2024)
- NYC gets **~200K transplants/year** who left their social networks behind
- The specific pain: *"I love tennis/surfing/hiking but I don't know anyone here who does it"*
- **47% of young adults** want more friends to do activities with (Bumble 2025)

### The Gap

Nobody does **"match me with a specific person, for a specific activity, at a specific time, near me."**

- Bumble BFF = person-first, no activity focus
- Meetup = large impersonal events, no matching, 30-50% no-show
- Timeleft = dinners only, proved the model works (€18M ARR)
- Strava = tracking, not meeting people

### Why It Could Fail

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Cold-start** (no users = no value) | CRITICAL | See Section 2 |
| **Retention cliff** (make 3 friends, leave) | HIGH | Recurring events, streaks, new sports discovery |
| **Dating app drift** | MEDIUM | Activity-first design, groups, no attractiveness signals |
| **Seasonal** | MEDIUM | Indoor activities for winter |

## 2. The Cold-Start Problem

### Why This Is THE Make-or-Break Issue

Social apps have one brutal rule: **if the app doesn't deliver value the FIRST time someone opens it, they delete it and never come back.** You get ONE shot.

If someone downloads ActiveCrew and sees:
- Zero people nearby → deleted
- 2 people but wrong sport → deleted
- Good match but no available times → deleted

**The app must feel alive and populated from minute one.**

### How to Solve It

#### Strategy 1: Don't Launch the App Until You Have Critical Mass

| Tactic | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Timeleft's 151-person threshold** | They wouldn't open a city until 151 people signed up. Copy this. Don't put ActiveCrew on the App Store until you have **200+ signups in one NYC neighborhood.** |
| **Waitlist landing page first** | Launch a beautiful one-page site: "ActiveCrew is coming to NYC. Sign up to be first." Collect emails + sport preferences. |
| **City/neighborhood counter** | "187/200 people in Williamsburg — 13 more and we launch!" Creates urgency. |

#### Strategy 2: Seed the App So It Never Feels Empty

| Tactic | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Pre-populated group events** | Before launch, create 10-15 group events (Rockaway surf, Central Park tennis, Bear Mountain hike). These show up even with zero users. |
| **Founder as power user** | YOU are the first 10 matches. You personally swipe yes on everyone, confirm plans, show up. For the first 2-3 weeks, you ARE the app. |
| **Recruit ambassadors** | Find 10-15 active, social NYC people (from run clubs, climbing gyms, surf groups). Give them early access. They seed the app with real profiles and real availability. |
| **Partner with existing groups** | DM 20 NYC Instagram sports accounts (@nycrunclub, etc.). "We're launching an app to help your members find training partners. Want to try it?" They bring 10-20 users each. |

#### Strategy 3: Guarantee a Good First Experience

| Tactic | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Concierge onboarding (first 100 users)** | When someone signs up, you personally text/DM them within 24 hours: "Hey! Welcome to ActiveCrew. I see you play tennis — I have 3 people near you who play at your level. Want me to set up a match?" Manual matching until the algorithm has enough data. |
| **"Your first match is guaranteed"** | Promise new users they'll get a match within 48 hours or you'll personally find them one. This is labor-intensive but only needed for the first 100-200 users. |
| **Show upcoming group events to everyone** | Even if 1-on-1 matching is thin, group events (Surf Saturday, Sunday Hike) always have spots. New users see immediate value. |

#### Strategy 4: Hyper-Local Launch

| Wrong | Right |
|-------|-------|
| Launch "NYC" (8 million people, nobody near each other) | Launch "Williamsburg" (dense, young, active, 2-mile radius) |
| 200 users spread across 5 boroughs | 200 users in one neighborhood |
| Matches are 45 minutes away by subway | Matches are 10 minutes away on foot |

**Rule of thumb: You need ~200 weekly active users within a 2-mile radius to generate reliable matches.**

## 3. How Timeleft Actually Makes Money

### The Key Insight: Timeleft Does NOT Pay Restaurants

This is the part most people misunderstand:

```
User pays Timeleft:  €13-26 ticket fee (or €15/month subscription)
User pays restaurant: their own food & drinks bill (separate, directly to restaurant)
Timeleft pays restaurant: NOTHING
```

**Timeleft's revenue is 100% ticket/subscription fees.** They have almost zero cost of goods sold.

### Why Restaurants Play Along (For Free)

- Timeleft fills tables on **Wednesday nights** — historically the slowest night for restaurants
- Restaurants get **guaranteed covers** (5-6 people who WILL order food and drinks)
- Average spend per person at dinner: **€25-40** on food/drinks
- That's **€125-240 per table** of guaranteed revenue the restaurant wouldn't have otherwise
- Timeleft sends **15,000+ people to restaurants every Wednesday** — restaurants are lining up to participate

### Timeleft's Unit Economics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Revenue per dinner ticket | €13-26 |
| Cost of goods sold | ~€0 (no food cost, no venue cost) |
| Gross margin | ~90%+ |
| Customer acquisition cost (CAC) | Estimated €5-15 via Meta ads |
| Lifetime value (LTV) | Estimated €50-100 (3-5 dinners avg) |
| Monthly participants | 150,000 |
| ARR | €18M |

### What This Means for ActiveCrew

**You also don't need to pay venues.** The model is the same:

- Tennis courts (public) → free, no venue cost
- Parks/trails (hiking, running) → free
- Beaches (surfing) → free
- Climbing gyms → user has their own membership
- Private courts/gyms → the user who's a member brings a guest

**ActiveCrew's revenue would be pure ticket/subscription fees with near-zero COGS, just like Timeleft.**

## 5. How ActiveCrew Makes Money — Per Sport

### The Core Question: What Are People Actually Paying For?

They're NOT paying for the sport itself (they can play tennis for free in Central Park). They're paying for:

1. **The match** — finding the right person at the right level
2. **The scheduling** — eliminating the "you free Thursday?" back-and-forth
3. **The trust** — reliability scores, verified profiles, safety
4. **The convenience** — one app, all sports, all plans

### Revenue by Sport Type

| Sport | Free/Paid Venue? | Booking Revenue Opportunity? | Monetization Strategy |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| **Tennis** | Public courts = free. Private courts = $30-80/hr | **YES — huge.** NYC public courts are a nightmare to book. If ActiveCrew can book courts, charge a $3-5 convenience fee. Partner with private clubs for discounted off-peak rates. | Subscription + court booking fee |
| **Running** | Free (parks, streets) | **NO** — no venue to book. But can charge for **group run events** ($10-15 for organized group run with pace groups + post-run coffee). | Subscription + group event tickets |
| **Cycling** | Free (roads, paths) | **Minimal** — but can partner with **bike shops** for group ride sponsorships. Can organize **paid group rides** ($15-20 for guided ride + brunch stop). | Subscription + sponsored events |
| **Hiking** | Free (trails) | **Minimal** — but can charge for **organized group hikes** ($15-25, includes transportation logistics). Many NYC hikers need help with transport to Bear Mountain, Breakneck Ridge, etc. Could partner with van/bus services. | Group event tickets + transport |
| **Surfing** | Free (beaches) | **Some** — can partner with **surf schools/board rentals** at Rockaway. "Book a board + find a surf buddy" = rental commission. | Subscription + rental partnerships |
| **Climbing** | Gym membership required ($100-200/mo) | **YES** — can offer **guest pass marketplace** or partner with gyms for day pass deals. Climbing gyms want new members. | Guest pass commission + gym partnerships |
| **Yoga** | Studios ($20-35/class) or free in parks | **YES** — can sell discounted **class credits** like ClassPass, but only for yoga meetups booked through ActiveCrew. | Class credit commission |
| **Basketball/Soccer** | Public courts = free | **Some** — can book **indoor courts** for organized pickup games ($15-25/person). | Group event tickets |
| **Gym/CrossFit** | Membership required | **YES** — guest passes, trial class deals with gyms. | Guest pass commission |

### The Money Matrix

```
HIGH BOOKING REVENUE          LOW BOOKING REVENUE
(venue needed)                (free venues)
                    
  Tennis courts               Running
  Climbing gyms               Cycling  
  Yoga studios                Hiking
  Indoor basketball           Surfing (free beach)
  
  → Booking fees              → Event tickets
  → Venue partnerships        → Subscription value
  → Guest pass commissions    → Sponsored experiences
```

### Where the REAL Money Is

| Revenue Stream | Potential Per User/Month | When to Introduce |
|---------------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Subscription** (core) | $10-15/mo | After 1,000 users |
| **Group event tickets** | $15-25/event × 2-3 events/mo = $30-75 | **From day 1** (like Timeleft) |
| **Court/venue booking fees** | $3-5 per booking × 4-8/mo = $12-40 | After venue partnerships (v2) |
| **Guest pass commissions** | 15-20% of $20-50 pass = $3-10 per pass | After gym partnerships (v2) |
| **B2B (ActiveCrew for Companies)** | $5-15/employee/mo | After brand recognition |

## 6. The 4 Revenue Models Analyzed

### Model 1: Freemium Subscription

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **How it works** | Free tier with limits (3 sports, 5 swipes/day, 2 matches/week). Premium unlocks unlimited + priority matching + advanced filters. |
| **Price** | $9.99-14.99/month |
| **Pros** | Recurring revenue. Predictable. Industry standard. Strava does $300M+/year with this. |
| **Cons** | Hard to convert free→paid (<5% typical conversion rate). Need massive free user base. |
| **Realistic for ActiveCrew?** | **YES — but not for v1.** Need 1,000+ free users before premium makes sense. |
| **Revenue at 5,000 users** | 5,000 × 5% conversion × $12/mo = **$3,000/mo** |

### Model 2: Per-Event Booking (Like Timeleft)

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **How it works** | Charge $10-25 per group event. User pays ActiveCrew to join a curated group activity. Free for 1-on-1 matching. |
| **Price** | $10-15 for group runs/hikes, $15-25 for surf days/organized activities |
| **Pros** | **Revenue from day 1.** Proves willingness to pay. Filters for serious users. Timeleft built €18M ARR on this. |
| **Cons** | Only works for group events, not 1-on-1 matching. Limits growth if priced too high. |
| **Realistic for ActiveCrew?** | **YES — START HERE.** This is how you validate the business before building subscription infrastructure. |
| **Revenue at 200 weekly participants** | 200 × $15/event × 4 weeks = **$12,000/mo** |

### Model 3: Ads

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **How it works** | Show ads from sports brands, gyms, equipment companies in the app feed. |
| **Price** | CPM-based: $5-15 per 1,000 impressions |
| **Pros** | Passive revenue. No friction on user experience (theoretically). |
| **Cons** | **Need 100K+ users to make meaningful money.** At 5,000 users, ads generate maybe $50-200/month. Ads kill trust in a social/safety app. Users hate them. Cheapens the brand. |
| **Realistic for ActiveCrew?** | **NO — skip entirely.** The math doesn't work below 100K users, and the brand damage isn't worth it. Raya, Timeleft, 222 — none use ads. Ads are for mass-market free apps (Facebook, TikTok), not curated communities. |
| **Revenue at 5,000 users** | Maybe **$100-300/mo** — not worth the brand damage |

### Model 4: Monthly Subscription (Raya Model — Exclusive)

| Aspect | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **How it works** | Everyone pays monthly to access the app. Application/invite required. No free tier. |
| **Price** | $9.99-19.99/month |
| **Pros** | Every user is a paying user. Creates exclusivity signal ("I'm a member"). Filters for committed people. Higher quality matches. Raya charges $20/mo with 2.5M waitlist. |
| **Cons** | **Massively limits growth.** Can't go viral if there's a paywall. Chicken-and-egg is 10x harder (need paying users before there's value). Only works with extreme demand/hype. |
| **Realistic for ActiveCrew?** | **NOT YET.** Raya works because celebrities use it. You don't have that pull yet. But could evolve toward this after building demand with a free model. |
| **Revenue at 500 paying members** | 500 × $15/mo = **$7,500/mo** |

### Recommended Revenue Strategy (Phased)

| Phase | Model | Why |
|-------|-------|-----|
| **v1 (Launch → 1,000 users)** | **Free matching + paid group events ($15-25)** | Proves willingness to pay. Generates revenue day 1. No friction for growth. |
| **v2 (1,000 → 5,000 users)** | **Add freemium subscription ($9.99-14.99/mo)** | Enough free users to convert a %. Premium = unlimited + priority matching. |
| **v3 (5,000+ users)** | **Add venue booking fees ($3-5/booking)** | Partner with tennis courts, climbing gyms. Take a cut of bookings. |
| **Never** | Ads | Not worth it. Kills trust. |

## 9. Timeleft Deep Dive

### Founder
**Maxime Barbier** — built MinuteBuzz (18M followers, 250M views/mo, 90 employees). Sold to TF1. Not a developer — built Timeleft entirely with no-code tools.

### Complete Timeline

```

2020 Sep  Formally co-founds with Adrien de Oliveira
2021      Seed round: FJ Labs + Kima Ventures (undisclosed, ~$500K-1M)

2023 Apr  Almost broke. 15-day test. Hacks Typeform + WhatsApp + Stripe.
2023 May 3  FIRST DINNER — 24 people in Lisbon. €125 revenue in week 1.
```

### Critical Lessons

1. **3 years of failed pivots** before finding product-market fit
2. **Validated with Typeform + WhatsApp + Stripe** — zero code
3. **Charged from day 1** — €125 first week
4. **Reached €1M/month using ONLY no-code tools** (Bubble + FlutterFlow)
5. **They tried activities matching in 2021-2022 and it failed** — too fragmented. Counter: sports is a tighter niche than "activities"
6. **Content creates itself** — every dinner = 6 people with phones
7. **60% women** — groups + public venues + curation = safety

## 20. Feature Analysis

### Affiliation Matching (Work/School)
**DO IT** — strongest anti-Tinder signal. "Find your tennis crew at Bloomberg" is clearly not dating. Viral within companies. Start as text field + filter. Cost: 1-2 weeks, $0.

### Venue Access (The Equinox Problem)
- **v1:** Guest pass badge ("🎟️ Can bring a guest") + suggest free alternatives. Cost: $0.
- **v2:** Court booking integration. Cost: $10-25K.
- **Skip:** ClassPass-style credits. That's a different business ($30-80K).

## 22. Retention

### Why Users Leave (and How to Keep Them)

People WILL exchange phone numbers after meeting. That's a success, not a failure. The goal: make the app more valuable than a phone number.

| Why They Stay | How |
|--------------|-----|
| New partners for different sports | Tennis buddy ≠ climbing partner |
| Skill-level matching | Phone number doesn't tell your level |
| Weekly planning convenience | Set availability once > text 5 people |
| Reliability scores | See someone's show-up rate before meeting |
| Group events | Coordination the app handles better than group texts |
| Discovery | Next week they want to try surfing with someone new |

### Tactics
- "Play Again" one-tap rebook
- Streak system ("4 weeks in a row with Marco 🔥")
- Progressive profile unlock after 3+ plans through app
- Seasonal challenges ("10 different sports this month")

## 24. Co-Founder Pitch Guide

### The Flow
1. Show the demo (2-3 min)
2. Drop the Timeleft stat ("€18M/year in 27 months from a Typeform")
3. Be specific about what he'd build (React Native, PostgreSQL, auth, chat, push, App Store)
4. Ask the key questions:

### Technical Fit Questions
- "Have you built a mobile app or worked with React/Node.js?"
- "Are you comfortable with full-stack — APIs, servers, deployment?"
- "How comfortable learning new frameworks on the fly?"

### Motivation Fit Questions
- "Does 'people can't find a tennis partner in NYC' feel like a real problem to you?"
- "15-20 hours/week for 3-4 months — are you in?"
- "What excites you more — building the product, or building the business?"
- "If this doesn't work after 6 months, how would you feel?"

### Green Flags
- "I literally tried to find a tennis partner last month"
- "Can I see the code?"
- "When do we start?"
- He starts suggesting features

### Red Flags
- "Sounds cool but I'm really busy"
- "I want 50/50 equity"
- "I don't really get why people would use this"

### Equity (Don't Offer Numbers in First Conversation)
- Full co-founder (15-20+ hrs/week): 25-40%
- Technical partner (10-15 hrs/week): 15-25%
- Part-time contributor (5-10 hrs/week): 5-15%
- Always: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff
